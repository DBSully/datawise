1. Executive Overview

DataWiseRE's fix-and-flip screening pipeline automates the initial deal qualification workflow that was previously performed manually in MS Access and Excel. The system processes properties through a six-stage calculation pipeline:

MLS Data Import | Comparable Search & Scoring -- find similar closed sales | ARV Calculation -- estimate after-repair value | Rehab Budget Estimation -- estimate renovation costs | Holding & Transaction Costs -- estimate carry and closing costs | Financing Costs -- hard money interest + origination | Deal Math & Qualification -- compute max offer, flag Prime Candidates

Design philosophy: The system is designed as an opportunity identification engine, not a precision appraisal tool. Its purpose is to rapidly surface properties with favorable deal characteristics so a human analyst can focus their time on the most promising opportunities. Every automated value can be overridden by the analyst.

Engine architecture: All calculation engines are pure functions with no database dependencies. They receive inputs and a strategy profile, and return structured results. The strategy profile (DENVER_FLIP_V1) centralizes every configurable parameter, ensuring engines contain zero hardcoded constants.

3. Comparable Selection & Scoring

Goal: Find the most similar recently-closed sales to a subject property, rank them by overall similarity, and present them for analyst selection. The comp engine is intentionally separated from the valuation engine — its output is a selected comp set, not a value.

3.2 Weighted Scoring System

Candidates passing hard filters receive a weighted composite score (0–100). Each component produces a 0–1 score, then all components are combined using purpose-specific weights.

Component Score Formula

componentScore = clamp(0, 1, 1 - delta / tolerance)

Where delta is the absolute or percentage difference, and tolerance is the maximum acceptable difference for that metric.

3.3 Haversine Distance Formula

a = sin²(Δlat/2) + cos(lat1) × cos(lat2) × sin²(Δlon/2) c = 2 × atan2(√a, √(1-a)) distance = 3,958.8 × c [miles]

Inputs: real_properties.latitude, real_properties.longitude for both subject and comp.

3.4 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Purpose-driven weight profiles (flip vs. rental vs. scrape) tailor comp selection to the analysis strategy.
  • Hard filters prevent irrelevant candidates from entering the scoring stage, keeping the comp pool focused.
  • Building form and level class matching go beyond basic bed/bath comparison, reflecting real property structure differences.
  • Full score breakdown is stored as JSON for transparency and analyst review.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • No neighborhood/subdivision awareness. Two properties 0.3 mi apart can be in very different neighborhoods. Consider adding subdivision match as a scoring component or allowing analysts to define neighborhood boundaries.
  • Condition scoring is coarse. Only exact match (1.0) vs. mismatch (0.3). The MLS condition field is often unreliable or missing. Consider supplementing with DOM (days on market) or price-change history as a proxy for condition.
  • No garage/feature adjustment. Garage count differences can materially affect value. Consider adding garage_spaces as a scoring component.
  • Binary lot size tolerance. A property at 29% delta passes; 31% is eliminated. Consider a gradual penalty ramp instead of a hard cutoff for fringe candidates.

4. ARV Calculations (After Repair Value)

Goal: Estimate the post-renovation market value of a subject property based on comparable closed sales, adjusting for size differences and time elapsed since each comp closed.

Source: lib/screening/arv-engine.ts

4.1 Per-Comp Adjusted ARV

Each comparable produces its own adjusted ARV estimate for the subject, computed in two layers (building-total and above-grade) then blended.

Step 1: Price Per Square Foot

psfBuilding = closePrice / compBuildingSqft psfAboveGrade = closePrice / compAboveGradeSqft

Inputs: mls_listings.close_price, property_physical.building_area_total_sqft, property_physical.above_grade_finished_area_sqft

Step 2: Size-Adjusted ARV (Two Layers)

arvBuilding = closePrice + (subjectBldgSqft - compBldgSqft) × psfBuilding × buildingDampening arvAboveGrade = closePrice + (subjectAboveSqft - compAboveSqft) × psfAboveGrade × aboveGradeDampening

Dampening factors (0–1) reduce the impact of size differences, recognizing that value does not scale linearly with square footage. For detached properties: building dampening = 0.3, above-grade dampening = 0.4.

Step 3: Blended ARV

arvBlended = arvBuilding × buildingTotalWeight + arvAboveGrade × aboveGradeWeight
Property TypeBuilding WeightAbove-Grade WeightBuilding DampeningAbove-Grade Dampening
Detached0.400.600.300.40
Condo0.150.850.200.45
Townhome0.350.650.300.40

Step 4: Time Adjustment

daysSinceClose = floor((referenceDate - closeDate) / 86,400,000) timeMultiplier = 1 + timeAdjustmentAnnualRate × (daysSinceClose / 365) arvTimeAdjusted = arvBlended × timeMultiplier

Current rate: -5% per year. A comp that closed 6 months ago receives a multiplier of 0.975 (2.5% downward adjustment).

Step 5: Confidence Score (Reference Only)

DistanceConfidence
0 – 0.3 mi1.0 (100%)
0.3 – 0.5 mi0.8 (80%)
0.5 – 0.6 mi0.6 (60%)
0.6 – 0.75 mi0.4 (40%)
> 0.75 mi0.2 (20%)

Note: Confidence scores are currently stored for analyst reference but are not used in the weighted ARV aggregation. The exponential decay weighting uses time, not distance.

4.2 Aggregate ARV (Exponential Decay Weighted Average)

Step 6: Decay Weight

decayWeight = e-(daysSinceClose / 365)

This naturally weights recent comps much more heavily:

1 month0.920Very high
3 months0.779High
6 months0.607Moderate
9 months0.472Low-moderate
12 months0.368Low

Step 7: Weighted Average

arvAggregate = Σ(arvTimeAdjustedi × decayWeighti) / Σ(decayWeighti) arvPerSqft = arvAggregate / subjectBuildingSqft

Output stored in: screening_results.arv_aggregate, screening_results.arv_per_sqft, screening_results.arv_comp_count, screening_results.arv_detail_json

Downstream consumers: Deal Math engine (max offer calculation), Qualification engine (Prime Candidate check), Analysis workstation UI (3-tier ARV display)

4.4 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Dual-layer valuation separates building-total from above-grade, properly handling basements and multi-level structures.
  • Dampening factors prevent extreme size adjustments from distorting value estimates.
  • Exponential decay is mathematically superior to linear time adjustments — older comps fade smoothly rather than falling off a cliff.
  • Property-type-aware blending correctly gives condos heavier above-grade weight (no exterior) while detached homes use more balanced weights.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • Fixed -5% annual time adjustment. This assumes a declining market. In an appreciating market, it understates ARV. Consider deriving this rate from actual market data (e.g., rolling 6-month median price change in the target area).
  • Confidence tiers are computed but unused. Distance-based confidence could be incorporated as a second weighting dimension (multiply decay weight × confidence) to favor closer comps more strongly.
  • No condition adjustment. A renovated comp and a dated comp at the same distance receive the same treatment. Consider adding a condition adjustment factor per comp.
  • No feature adjustments beyond size. Garage, lot size, bed/bath differences are captured in scoring but not in ARV adjustment. These are secondary but could be added as optional refinements.
  • No outlier detection. A single comp with an extreme price could skew the aggregate. Consider flagging comps whose per-comp ARV deviates >20% from the median.

5.4 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Multiplicative system is elegant and tunable. Each dimension (type, condition, price, age) acts independently, making it easy to adjust one factor without cascading effects.
  • New construction handling (multiplier = 0) correctly zero-outs rehab for brand-new homes.
  • Property-type differentiation in both rates and multipliers reflects real market behavior (condos don't need exterior/landscaping).
  • Separate below-grade finished/unfinished rates properly account for different renovation scopes.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • Static base rates. $35/sqft is a Denver-specific number from a point in time. Consider adding a rate inflation adjustment or tying rates to a construction cost index.
  • MLS condition field is unreliable. Only "fixer" triggers a non-default multiplier. Consider supplementing with photo analysis, DOM, or price-to-comp-ratio as condition proxies.
  • No renovation scope differentiation. A light cosmetic flip and a full gut-job both start from the same base rate. Consider adding scope tiers (cosmetic / moderate / heavy / gut) that the analyst can select, each with different base rate adjustments.
  • Below-grade unfinished rate ($49/sqft) is higher than finished ($39/sqft). This reflects the cost of finishing unfinished space (not just cosmetic updates). This is correct for a flip that finishes the basement, but could overstate costs if the flip plan leaves the basement as-is. Consider making basement finishing optional.
  • No contingency/overhead factor. Professional estimators typically add 10–15% for unknowns. Consider adding an optional contingency multiplier.

6.3 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Size-scaled holding period is a practical innovation — larger homes genuinely take longer to renovate and sell.
  • Real data where available (actual tax and HOA from MLS) is better than estimates.
  • Minimum floor prevents unrealistically short hold periods for small properties.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • No financing costs. Most flippers use hard money loans (10–14% annual interest). This is the single largest missing cost component. The financingEnabled flag exists in the profile but is not yet implemented. Prioritize this.
  • Insurance based on list price, not ARV. Insurance during renovation is typically based on the intended completed value or purchase price. Using list price is reasonable for screening but could understate costs for properties listed below market.
  • No seasonal adjustment. Properties listed in winter may take longer to sell. Consider adding a seasonal factor or simply flagging this as an analyst consideration.
  • Tax/HOA default to zero when null. If financial data is missing from the MLS, the holding cost is understated. Consider using a market-average fallback for missing tax data.
  • Utility rate is flat. $0.08/sqft/month is a rough average. Vacant properties during renovation use less but may have minimum service charges.

7.2 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Clean separation of acquisition vs. disposition costs.
  • Commission based on ARV (the expected sale price) rather than list price is correct.
  • Rates are configurable in the strategy profile.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • Disposition title should be based on ARV, not list price. Title and closing costs on the sell side are a function of the sale price (ARV), not the original purchase price. This currently understates disposition closing costs.
  • No transfer tax / recording fees. Colorado has relatively low transfer taxes, but some jurisdictions charge additional fees. Consider adding a per-transaction flat fee component.
  • Commission rate may be high post-NAR settlement. The 4% disposition commission assumes the seller pays the buyer's agent. Post-2024 NAR settlement, this structure is changing. Consider separating listing agent commission (2–3%) from buyer agent commission (negotiable).
  • No acquisition commission. If buying through an agent, there may be buyer-side commission or fees. Consider adding an optional acquisition commission rate.

7.5 Financing Cost Calculations

7.5.3 Assessment

Strengths:
  • ARV-based loan sizing is the correct approach for hard money — it breaks the circular dependency and matches how lenders actually underwrite.
  • All three key parameters are overridable per deal, allowing analysts to model different financing scenarios.
  • Interest-only model is appropriate for short-term hard money (flippers don't make principal payments).
  • Origination included as a one-time upfront cost correctly reflects how points work in practice.
Weaknesses & Future Considerations:
  • No draw schedule / rehab holdback modeling. Many hard money lenders fund rehab in draws, meaning not all loan proceeds are disbursed day one. Interest on rehab draws could be modeled separately for more accuracy.
  • No prepayment penalty modeling. Some hard money loans have minimum interest periods (e.g., 3 months guaranteed interest). Consider adding a minimum interest floor.
  • Cash-to-close not yet computed. Consider adding: cashToClose = purchasePrice - loanAmount + originationCost + acquisitionTitle to show the equity requirement.
  • No comparison with cash purchase. Consider showing financing vs. all-cash deal economics side-by-side to help analysts evaluate leverage decisions.

8.3 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Clean, transparent waterfall makes it easy for analysts to see exactly how the max offer is derived.
  • Target profit is overridable per deal, allowing flexibility for different risk profiles.
  • Gap/sqft normalization enables apples-to-apples comparison across different-sized properties.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • Target profit is a flat dollar amount. Consider offering a percentage-of-ARV option (e.g., 10% of ARV) as an alternative, since larger deals typically require larger absolute returns.
  • No ROI or annualized return calculation. $40,000 profit on a $320,000 investment over 190 days is very different from $40,000 on a $150,000 investment over 90 days. Consider adding: ROI = profit / totalInvestment and annualizedROI = ROI × (365 / daysHeld).

9. Prime Candidate Qualification

Goal: Identify properties with the strongest deal characteristics — those where multiple nearby, recent comps independently support a significant value gap.

Source: lib/screening/qualification-engine.ts

9.1 Qualification Rules

9.2 Assessment

Strengths:
  • Multi-comp confirmation is the key innovation — requiring 2+ independent qualifying comps dramatically reduces false positives.
  • Per-comp gap analysis (not just aggregate) prevents a single outlier comp from triggering Prime status.
  • Tighter distance/recency requirements than general screening ensure Prime Candidates have strong local evidence.
  • Stored reasons and disqualifiers give the analyst full transparency.
Weaknesses & Suggestions:
  • $60/sqft gap threshold is market-specific. In a lower-cost market, $60/sqft may be unrealistic. Consider making this a percentage of comp ARV (e.g., 10%) as an alternative or supplementary threshold.
  • No consideration of deal feasibility. A property with a $60/sqft gap but an ARV of $200,000 may not be worth flipping due to thin absolute margins. Consider adding a minimum absolute spread check.
  • Time since listing not considered. A property listed yesterday vs. 90 days ago has different negotiation dynamics. Stale listings may offer better negotiation opportunities.
